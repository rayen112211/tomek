

## PROJECT SPECIFICATION
Account Based Marketing (ABM)
## Qmatch Consulting
Working draft – April 2026

## 1. Project Context
Qmatch Consulting is an advisory company specializing in organizational transformation, building
management structures, and helping business owners break free from the so-called “owner’s trap.”
We work with companies experiencing rapid growth to streamline their processes, build leadership
teams, and prepare the organization for scaling.
Until now, our client acquisition has relied primarily on referrals and inbound activity. We are
launching  an  ABM  (Account  Based  Marketing)  project  aimed  at  proactively  reaching  out  to
companies that meet specific criteria and engaging decision-makers – primarily via email, and
where appropriate, by phone.
As part of this project, we are looking for someone to build us a system / toolset for identifying and
acquiring leads – from finding companies that match our criteria, through identifying decision-
makers, to obtaining their verified contact details. This document describes what we expect and
what the end result should look like.

- What We Want to Achieve
## 2.1. Primary Goal: A Lead Generation System
The most important outcome of this project is a working system (tool / toolset) that allows us to
search for companies based on defined criteria, identify decision-makers, and obtain their contact
details – in a repeatable and relatively straightforward way. We want someone from our team to be
able to sit down, use the system, and find leads based on a given specification – much faster and
easier than doing it manually.
The first batch of leads (a ready-made database) should be treated as a proof of concept – evidence
that the system works and delivers what we need. The main deliverable, however, is the system
itself, not a one-off database.
## 2.2. Expected System Capabilities
-  Ability to search for companies based on configurable criteria (revenue, growth, industry,
legal form, etc.)
-  Identification of decision-makers within the companies found
-  Retrieval of contact details (email, LinkedIn, optionally phone) – see Section 2.4
-  Lead scoring / quality assessment (nice-to-have)
-  Export of results (CSV, Excel, or a panel/dashboard)
-  Ability to expand with new target groups and criteria in the future

2.3. Data Requirements (Must-Have)
Each lead delivered by the system should include:
-  Company name, industry, approximate annual revenue, and headcount
-  Short description of the company’s business (e.g. what the company does, what products
or services it offers) – this helps us craft more personalized email outreach
-  Full name(s) and job title(s) of the decision-maker(s)
-  Decision-maker’s email address – verified and up-to-date (see Section 2.4)
-  Ability to filter and sort results (e.g. by industry, company size, score)
2.4. Email Sourcing and Verification
Decision-maker email addresses are the single most critical element of this entire project. We want
to minimize empty fields – the goal is to have contact details for as many identified individuals as
possible. That’s why the approach to emails should encompass three complementary methods:
- Lookup in existing databases and tools – e.g. Hunter, Apollo, publicly available online
sources. This is the first step: check whether the address is already available somewhere.
- Verification of discovered addresses – just because a tool returns an email address
doesn’t mean it’s current or active. Every address found should be verified (e.g. using
email verification tools such as ZeroBounce, NeverBounce, etc.).
- Bulk validation / email guessing – if an address can’t be found in any source, it can
often be discovered based on a pattern. In Poland, the most common format is: first letter
of the first name + last name @ company domain (e.g. jkowalski@firma.pl). Variations
exist (firstname.lastname, lastname.firstname, etc.), but there are tools for bulk-validating
such combinations. This way, we can “discover” an address even without access to any
database.
Important: we do not want a missing address to simply mean an empty field in the database. If the
first method doesn’t work, apply the second, then the third. The goal is maximum coverage –
contact details for every identified decision-maker.
2.5. Nice-to-Have
-  Decision-maker’s phone number
-  Additional company information: ownership structure, history of changes in KRS (Polish
company register), headcount trends
-  Automated detection of growth signals (e.g. year-over-year revenue growth, hiring activity)
-  Information on the company’s media presence / the owner’s activity on LinkedIn
-  A brief rationale explaining why the company fits our ICP (e.g. “+80% YoY revenue
growth, owner on the board, no COO in place”)
-  Decision-maker’s LinkedIn profile

## 4. Polish Business Context
Since the Polish market may still be relatively new to you, we’ve compiled the key specifics that will
help you in your work.
4.1. Legal Forms of Companies
We are exclusively targeting “spółka z o.o.” (limited liability companies) – this is the most common
legal form for mid-sized companies in Poland. The English equivalent is a limited liability company
## (LLC).
-  Not relevant: “jednoosobowa działalność gospodarcza” (sole proprietorships / JDG) – too
small for our purposes
-  “Spółki akcyjne” (joint-stock companies / S.A.) are also generally out of scope – these are
typically large corporations that already have established management structures
## 4.2. Available Data Sources
In Poland, public registries provide financial data on limited liability companies (annual financial
statements are publicly available). Below are a few sources that may be useful – treat them as a
starting point and inspiration, not as a constraint. If you know of better tools or have your own
approach – great, what matters to us is the end result.
- Rejestr.io (rejestr.io) – an aggregator of data from KRS and financial statements; offers
a paid API with filtering by revenue, industry, headcount, and growth dynamics
- KRS (Krajowy Rejestr Sądowy / National Court Register) – the official company
register. Here you can find data on management boards, shareholders, and PKD codes
(industry classification)
- BiznesRadar / Aleo.com – business intelligence portals with company financials
- GUS (Główny Urząd Statystyczny / Central Statistical Office) – statistical data on
companies

If access to any API requires a paid subscription, we can allocate budget for that – we’ll just need
a cost estimate for those solutions. We prefer cost-effective options where possible, but if a paid
tool is significantly better, that’s not a problem.
4.3. Reaching Decision-Makers
In the Polish context, key decision-makers are typically:
-  The owner (shareholder who also serves on the management board)
-  The CEO (Prezes Zarządu)
-  A board member (Członek Zarządu)
A practical approach involves combining: KRS data (board composition) + LinkedIn (professional
profiles) + email sourcing tools.
Based on our experience, it’s also worth considering alternative contact paths, especially when
reaching the owner directly is difficult:
- Executive assistant / assistant to the management board – in many companies this
contact has proven highly effective. Assistants have direct access to the owner/CEO and
can efficiently pass along a relevant topic if it’s well framed.
- Family members (same last name) – particularly in family-owned businesses, it’s
common that reaching the owner directly is hard, but other people with the same last
name work in different positions within the company – often family members (son,
daughter, brother, nephew, etc.). They tend to be younger, more open to external
outreach, and can forward the topic along – to the father, uncle, or brother who is the
actual decision-maker.
These are just suggestions based on our experience. If you have a better approach, we’re open to
it.
